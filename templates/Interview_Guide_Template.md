## Opening (5 minutes)

### Introduction and Consent

**Script:**

"Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today. My name is [Name] and I'm working with [Organization] on [general purpose - be vague about solution].

Before we begin, I want to explain how this conversation will work:

1. **Purpose**: We're trying to understand [problem space] better. We're in the discovery phase and haven't built anything yet, so there's nothing to sell you.

2. **Confidentiality**: Everything you share will be kept confidential. We may use anonymized quotes (like "a rural clinic director said...") but never attribute them to you by name without permission.

3. **Recording** (if applicable): With your permission, I'd like to record this conversation so I can focus on our discussion rather than taking notes. The recording is for internal use only. Is that okay with you?
   - [ ] Permission granted
   - [ ] Permission declined (take detailed notes)

4. **Time**: We have about 45-60 minutes. If we run short on time, I'll prioritize the most important questions.

5. **Your questions**: At any point, feel free to ask me questions or clarify anything.

Do you have any questions before we start?"

## Section 1: Context and Background (10 minutes)

**Objective:** Understand interviewee's role, responsibilities, and how they encounter the problem space.

### Q1: Role and Responsibilities

"Can you start by telling me about your role and what a typical day or week looks like for you?"

**Listen for:**
- Specific tasks and workflows
- Pain points that emerge naturally
- Time spent on different activities
- Decision-making authority
- Who they work with

**Probe if needed:**
- "Walk me through a recent [relevant activity]"
- "How much of your time is spent on [relevant area]?"
- "Who else is involved in this process?"

"Tell me a bit about [their organization] - size, structure, who you serve?"

**Listen for:**
- Organization size and resources
- Budget constraints or procurement process
- Regulatory or policy environment
- Culture and decision-making process

## Section 2: Problem Discovery (15 minutes)

**Objective:** Validate the problem exists, understand its significance, identify triggers and frequency.

### Q3: Problem Exploration (Open-Ended)

"We've heard from others in your position that [general problem area] can be challenging. What's your experience with this?"

**CRITICAL:** Do NOT mention your solution. Keep it about their experience.

**Listen for:**
- Confirmation or denial of problem
- Specific examples and stories
- Emotional signals (frustration, resignation, urgency)
- Impact on their work or organization
- Who else is affected

**Probe:**
- "Can you give me a specific example?"
- "When was the last time this happened?"
- "How often does this occur?"
- "What's the impact when this happens?"

**Notes:**

---

### Q4: Specific Incident

"Tell me about the last time [problem] happened. Walk me through what occurred."

**Listen for:**
- Triggering event
- Their response and workaround
- Time spent on workaround
- Outcome (resolved, unresolved, escalated)
- Cost (time, money, quality, relationships)

**Notes:**

---

### Q5: Frequency and Magnitude

"How often do you encounter [problem]? Is it daily, weekly, monthly?"

"On a scale of 1-5, where 1 is a minor annoyance and 5 is a critical issue, how would you rate this problem?"

**Listen for:**
- Specific frequency (not "often" but "3 times per week")
- Consistency (always, seasonal, random)
- Trend (getting worse, stable, improving)
- Quantifiable impact (hours lost, revenue impact, patient harm)

**Notes:**

---

### Q6: Current Workarounds

"What do you do today when [problem] occurs? Walk me through your current process."

**Listen for:**
- Step-by-step workaround
- Tools or people involved
- Time and cost of workaround
- Workaround success rate
- Why workaround is inadequate

**Notes:**

## Section 3: Needs Extraction (15 minutes)

**Objective:** Elicit specific, measurable needs without solution priming.

### Q7: Ideal Outcome

"If you could wave a magic wand and solve [problem] perfectly, what would that look like? What would change?"

**CRITICAL:** Let them describe the outcome, not the solution.

**Listen for:**
- Measurable outcomes (time saved, errors reduced, faster response)
- Priorities (what matters most)
- Constraints (what can't change)
- Success criteria

**Probe:**
- "What would success look like?"
- "How would you measure improvement?"
- "What's the minimum improvement that would matter?"

**Notes:**

---

### Q8: Needs Prioritization

"If you could only improve ONE thing about [problem area], what would it be and why?"

**Listen for:**
- Clear prioritization
- Rationale (why this over others)
- Urgency indicators

**Notes:**

---

### Q9: Importance vs. Satisfaction

For each need identified, ask:

"On a scale of 1-5, how important is [need] to your work?"
"On a scale of 1-5, how satisfied are you with current solutions for [need]?"

## Section 4: Solution Landscape (10 minutes)

**Objective:** Understand existing alternatives and competitive positioning.

### Q10: Current Solutions

"What tools, products, or services do you currently use to address [problem]? What do you like about them? What's missing?"

**Listen for:**
- Specific competitors or alternatives
- What works well (don't compete here)
- What doesn't work (opportunity)
- Switching barriers
- Pricing and procurement

**Probe:**
- "Why did you choose [current solution]?"
- "What would make you switch to something else?"
- "What have you tried in the past that didn't work?"

**Notes:**

---

### Q11: Competitive Benchmarking

"If I told you there was a solution that could [describe value prop WITHOUT specifics], would that be interesting to you? Why or why not?"

**Listen for:**
- Immediate interest or skepticism
- Objections (these reveal constraints)
- Comparison to current solutions

**IMPORTANT:** Still don't describe your specific solution. Stay high-level.

**Notes:**

---

## Section 5: Acceptability and Ecosystem (5 minutes)

**Objective:** Identify stakeholders, policies, and adoption barriers.

### Q12: Decision-Making and Approval

"If you wanted to adopt a new [tool/service/approach] to address [problem], what would that process look like? Who else would need to be involved?"

**Listen for:**
- Budget authority and procurement process
- Other stakeholders (IT, compliance, leadership)
- Approval timeline
- Regulatory or policy constraints
- Pilot vs. full deployment requirements

**Notes:**

---

### Q13: Ecosystem Actors

"Who else has a stake in [problem area]? Who would need to support or approve this?"

**Listen for:**
- Enablers (help you succeed)
- Resistors (block or slow adoption)
- Influencers (shape opinions)
- Payers (distinct from users if applicable)

**Notes:**

---

## Section 6: Willingness to Pay (5 minutes)

**Objective:** Test pricing assumptions and understand value perception.

### Q14: Value Quantification

"If a solution could [specific benefit validated earlier], what would that be worth to you or your organization?"

**Listen for:**
- Dollar value if they provide it
- Time savings valued in $
- Risk reduction valued in $
- Comparison to current spending

**Notes:**

---

### Q15: Pricing Bracket Test (Van Westendorp Method)

"I'm going to ask you about pricing, but remember we're just exploring, nothing is for sale today."

**Ask these four questions:**

1. "At what price would you consider this too expensive to consider?" (Too Expensive)
   - Answer: $_____

2. "At what price would you consider it expensive, but still worth considering?" (Expensive but Acceptable)
   - Answer: $_____

3. "At what price would you consider it a bargain - a great deal?" (Bargain)
   - Answer: $_____

4. "At what price would you consider it too cheap that you'd question the quality?" (Too Cheap)
   - Answer: $_____

**Range:** [Too Cheap] to [Too Expensive]
**Optimal Price Zone:** [Expensive but Acceptable] to [Bargain]

**Notes:**

## Section 7: Validation and Pilot Interest (5 minutes)

**Objective:** Gauge interest in co-creation, pilot participation, or early adoption.

### Q16: Pilot Interest

"We're planning to pilot an approach to [problem] in the next [timeframe]. Would you be interested in participating if it were available?"

**Listen for:**
- Enthusiasm level (1-5)
- Conditions for participation
- Timeline constraints
- Decision-making requirements

**Follow-up if interested:**
- "What would success look like for you in a pilot?"
- "What would make you a reference customer for others?"
- "What concerns would you have about piloting something new?"
